There’s BV and then there’s BCV

In most agile shops, we’ve all heard about Business Value (BV), but there’s no solid and mature model for helping to understand why a project should be prioritized above or below the next. We developed a flexible model that’s easily embedded in your existing processes and frameworks.

Estimating business value needs to be flexible, and adapt to the intent of the business objectives. The idea is to tie portions of human work to their respective value streams, to enhance trade off discussions, but we know we can’t make that translation yet.

Let’s look at a business from the 30,000 ft view. When you look at a SWOT model, you see that we make decisions based on potential opportunities, and potential threats. Otherwise stated as business values and risks.

A value needs to be created that is quickly understood that represents both value and risk. Without one or the other, part of the holistic picture will be missing from the discussion. We’ve come up with BCV is the result of policy driven objectives to enhance trade off discussions

It’s important to measure value if you’re looking at risks, mostly because there’s directly related, but also from a holistic perspective.

A game isn’t played on offense alone; only with a solid and complementary defense can a win be tallied. We need to understand both the potential and the risks we face at all times.

Business Criticality Value

BCV, like story points, is modular and is a policy driven estimate applied to significant efforts . Story Points estimate effort + doubt + uncertainty, and by nature include risk in terms of doubt and uncertainty. Risk is also part of the BCV assessment, while the inputs are defined and maintained by the business.

Why do we need BCV, and where do you stick it?

BCV was the solution devised in fast growing IT group, and was needed to drive mutual understanding within the IT Steering Committee.

It’s simply tally of a series of questions about the impact of the effort to the business in the following terms, and estimated as small, medium or large. The t-shirt sizes are given a value of 1, 2 or 3 and totaled.

  • positive impact to the customer
  • how many customers will is impact
  • strategic value to the business as a whole
  • risk of contract or compliance requirements
  • risk of missing a deadline or market opportunity
  • risk to system health or applications

These are estimated and represented to all the business leaders to help them understand and compare what the business is getting from the project or effort. The methodology behind BCV has its roots with the financial risk measure of VaR which indicates potential exposure to risk within a certain time constraint. It also pulls from the statistical predictor of value to the business in various terms.

Where do story points fit in?

Story points are nothing new to most agile shops, and in most places there is some level of standardization. Larger efforts that don’t fit into a sprint will need to be estimated before we can calculate an ROI value that makes any kind of sense.

Return On Investment (ROI)

The “biggest bang for the buck”. We’ve all heard it. But what is it?

Biggest “bang” refers to what we expect in return from our efforts, and “buck” refers to how much that effort costs.

BCV is the “bang” or value and Story Points are the “buck” or cost.

With BCV / Points you get an ROI ratio that can be used to enhance the value and risks for each project from a company wide objective, and expose it to enhance and expedite trade off discussions. We’ve had good luck translating the numeric value to a color scheme, and display color markers on each project card.
